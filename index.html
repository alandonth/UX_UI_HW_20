What'sHapp

A spontaneous travel planning made for your adventurous side.

In the beginning

What'sHapp was like any other travel app. A place for the user to organize dates and set itineraries with the added ability to see events or attractions taking place in specific areas. We knew there had to be more, so we researched.







Research

"I'm the type of person that can plan out 60% of the trip and purposely leave 40% of it unplanned to be spontaneous. "

-Vishal P. (interviewee)

Research Plan

Our research began by conducting face to face interviews in search of qualitative data as well as distributing a survey for more quantitative data. We asked questions that gave us valuable insight into the mindset of modern travelers. After we listened, we began to analyze and synthesize our data utilizing our user insight board, affinity diagram, and empathy map. It wasn't long before we discovered that our users weren't looking for a way to plan their vacations but almost a way to "not plan out all of their vacations".

Definition/Ideation

People do not have the time or desire to research and plan out their vacations but want assurance that where they go will be worth it. How might we make our users less stressed and more confident in their next travel plans while maintaining freedom and flexibility throughout the process?

Definition/Ideation

After analyzing our research findings we established a user persona that best represented our common user. Remember that idea I mentioned before, that our users were looking for a way to "not plan out their vacations"? Well we realized that people don't want their vacation to feel like work. They want the freedom and flexibility in their schedule to explore and be spontaneous while still taking comfort in the fact that what they book will be worth it. So we dove deeper into that and came out the other side with a goal-directing problem statement.

Storyboard

After multiple iterations we landed on a storyboard that accurately displayed our users common pains as well as satisfied their wants and needs.

User Journey Map

Then we followed it up with a user journey map to better identify opportunities throughout the process that WhatsHapp could target and solve for.

I Like, I Wish, What If

Next we employed this classic technique to identify areas of the vacation planning process that our users currently liked, wish they had, and dreamt of having in the future.

Converge

From there we began to converge and identify which features were worth pursuing based on our users wants and current capabilities. We liked the idea of creating an app that allowed our users to be spontaneous. The last "what if" sticky really stuck with us - what if the user could embark on a trip without knowing any (or maybe most) of the details up front. Why not? That certainly would be spontaneous.

Prototyping

Main Feature Task Flow

Before creating any sketches or screens we first established a basic task flow for the surprise me feature. This feature allows user to choose from a variety of locations to visit after inputing available dates. Once the date arrives users are prompted to choose a specific style of adventure from sightseeing to rockclimbing and everything in between.

From here it was time to get it into the hands of users.

Testing

Guerrilla Testing

After conducting guerrilla user tests, the insights gathered were organized into 4 main categories and grouped based on the frequency of responses.

Iterated User Flow

Based on the insights gathered, the user flow was iterated on to more accurately solve the users needs.

Solution

Iterated Solution

After iterating the user flow, the wireframes were iterated on to reflect the updated flow. Additionally UI components were added to increase the fidelity of the prototype.

Always be listening.

Users have specific needs. Don't try to solve a problem they don't have. After interviewing users and analyzing survey data, it was clear that the original intent of providing the user with a simple vacation planner wasn't helping them out. Users lacked the time and motivation to do the planning to begin with. Our users wanted something that will help them enjoy their vacations more, not add more stress.

Always be iterating.

Iterating was key to all of our significant progress. Coming up with ideas and solutions in isolation led to nowhere, but implementing, testing, and analyzing feedback helped to refine the solution to one that more acacurately addresses the user's needs.
